1. What is a Belief and Why Is This Question Worth Asking?

We all believe things. We believe many things. In this sense, it is a shared experience. To understand what a belief is, what they are for, how they can be gained, changed, lost, dropped and used, may illuminate some facet of us human, believing beings. A view of this facet, might grant more us empathy for one another, or aiding in our vigilance against truly anti-human beliefs (if such a thing could ever be determined).


1a. What Do I Expect To Do With The Conclusion?

I hope to gain some insight into my own beliefs, where they come from, what forces change them or maintain them. I hope, through that lense, to better understand my friends and family and collegues and neighbors. To have a minute glimpe into: how they think; or how make decisions; or why they do what they do. 

More broadly I am probably hoping to gain some insight into "how the world works" or rather why it's working like _this_, so seemingly badly. We are at each others throats. I suspect that one factor in this is that we all think we know what others believe and have judged their beliefs incorrect, or dangerous, or bad. But I believe we don't know _what_ we believe much of the time. We only _believe_ that we know.

If any of this is true, maybe sharing this insight will help someone, somewhere _see_ their fellow and judge them without haste, because they have a certain empathy about the difficulty or mystery of belief.



2. What Do I Think About Belief?

One's beliefs are like a collection of totems. They are things we hold and they can be deeply important to us. We hold them close. And though we usually act with a belief in hand, it has no agency of its own. But we do wield them, knowingly or not. I think it's better to know that we wield them. Perhaps we don't need to know which belief is in hand all of the time. Perhaps it's enough to recognize their presence, their weight, and their practical use.


2a. What Can Be Done With A Belief?

Totems can be given, gifted, rejected, formed, shaped, morphed, dropped, left behind, smashed to pieces, ignored, and hidden. We probably do all these things with our beliefs. And a belief, just like a totem, doesn't have to represent a _real_ thing. It could depict a cat or it could depict a chimera. It could depict an iguana or it could depict a dragon. The totem is real in either case and it's important to recognize that. That is because seeing the shape of something imparts an image. It frames one's reality. An image limits reality, or expands it, or clarifies it. Likewise, a belief can imply, to the believer, what's possible and what's not possible. It implies what is good and what is bad. Itimplies what is real or surreal or unreal. It implies what is true and what is false. Beliefs can be in harmony or conflict with eachother.

As one person relates to another, beliefs are also tools. They can be used to build relationships and communities, out of which real buildings, and services, and support systems, and opportunities, and innovations are made. They have utility, They are practical for the human and for the society. But tools can fall into disrepair, or be unwieldy, or be dangerous, needing caution, needing instruction.


2b. How Do We Relate To Beliefs?

We have a lot of beliefs, totems. More than we know. Many of them we inherit and lay, unexamined, in an attic. Some sit in view on the family bookshelf or hang like a picture on the wall. Some are forgotten, lodged deep between kitchen cabinets. Beliefs can be found anywhere. Some we are given at school. Some emerge from the pages of a book or the images on a screen. Some lay very close to our body. Some are hidden in the lining of our coat, fallen through the hole of well-worn pocket. We all believe things. We believe many things. We are not aware of every belief we possess, let alone what beliefs others possess. But we move through the world with them regardless.


2c. How Should We Relate To Beliefs?

There are times and places in human life where beliefs are more important, whey they are weilded in ceremony or clung to in expectation. Religious practices and rituals are, perhaps, most obvious. It may help to notice where else or when else a belief is present. What if we caught ourselves in the act belief? Girded with such a practice, we might recognize the act of belief in others. With such experience, we might give grace to them in conflict, the same grace we extend to ourselves. We might recieve delight in the harmony of mutual beliefs, the same delight we recieve while listening to a moving speech.
